Introduction

Introduction

Consider an asset return time series:

\[y_t=\mu+\epsilon_t\]

  • \(\mu\) is the expected return

\[\mu=E(y_t|F_{t-1})\]

  • \(\epsilon_t\) is the innovation at time \(t\)
    • Serially uncorrelated, but serially dependent
    • Can exhibit time-varying variances
      • Conditional Heteroscedasticity

\[\sigma^2=Var(\epsilon_t|F_{t-1})\]

Volatility

  • The conditional standard deviation of the asset returns
    • Not directly observable
  • Measures how much the return fluctuates around the mean
    • A measure of risk

Financial Time Series

  • Heavy tails
    • Excess kurtosis
    • Higher probability of having extreme events than normally distributed case
  • Volatility clustering
    • A large \(\epsilon_{t-1}^2\) tends to be followed by another large \(\epsilon_t^2\)
    • Shows periods of high volatility and periods of low volatility

Time Series Models

ARMA:

  • The conditional standard deviation given the past is constant

Volatility models:

  • Assume a specific parametric form for the conditional heteroskedasticity
  • Model the non-constant time-varying volatility
    • To compute the volatility at any instant
    • To analyze how it varies over time
    • To forecast its future values

Volatility Models

GARCH

  • Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity
  • \(\epsilon_t\) follows a GARCH(p,q) model if

\[\epsilon_t=\sqrt{h_t}w_t\] \[h_t = \omega + \sum_{i=1}^{q}\alpha_i \epsilon_{t-i}^2 + \sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_j h_{t-j}\]

  • \(w_t\) is a sequence of i.i.d. unit variance white noise process and assumed to follow:
    • Standard normal distribution
    • Student Distribution
    • Generalized Error Distribution (GED)
  • GARCH(1,1) is usually the best to fit financial time series

GARCH-M

  • M stands for in the mean
  • Includes volatility as a regression variable in the mean equation directly

  • GARCH(1,1)-M: \[ \begin{align*} y_t &= \mu + c(\sqrt{h_t})^k + \epsilon_t, \\ \epsilon_t &= \sqrt{h_t}w_t, \\ h_t &= \omega + \alpha_1 \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta_1 h_{t-1} \end{align*} \]

  • \(c\) : the risk premium
    • Expected to be positive
    • The return is positively related to its volatility
    • \(k=1\): Conditional standard deviation
    • \(k=2\): Conditional variance

Stochastic Volatility

  • Assumes the logarithm of the squared volatility of returns follows a latent autoregressive process of order one

\[ \begin{align*} y_t &= e^{h_t/2}\epsilon_t,\\ h_{t} &= \mu+\phi(h_{t-1}-\mu)+\sigma\eta_t\\ \end{align*} \]

, where \(\epsilon_t\) and \(\eta_t\) are uncorrelated i.i.d. standard normal white noise processes

  • \(h_t\) is the latent log-volatility
    • follows a stationary process \((|\phi|<1)\)
  • The volatility of \(y_t\) is

\[\sqrt{Var(y_t|F_{t-1})}=\exp(h_t/2)\]

Model Building

Steps

Building a volatility model for an asset return time series consists of the following steps:

  1. Specify a mean equation
  2. Test for ARCH effects
  3. Specify a volatility model
  4. Check the fitted model

Example

The daily log returns of S&P 500 from 03/01/1986 to 31/12/2015

1. Specify a Mean Equation

  • Build an ARMA model for the mean equation to remove any linear dependence

  • Both tail off to zero after lag 2
  • Specify an ARMA(1,1) model to remove the linear dependence

  • Use the squared residuals of the mean equation to test for conditional heteroscedasticity

Model Building: GARCH

1. Joint Estimation of ARMA(p,q)-GARCH(1,1)

  • Assume normal innovations
  • ARMA(p,q) with \(p\in\{0,1,2\}\) and \(q\in\{0,1,2\}\) are fitted for the mean equation
  • Models with all statistically significant coefficients:
    • GARCH(1,1)
    • ARMA(1,1)-GARCH(1,1)
    • ARMA(2,2)-GARCH(1,1)

2. Fit Other Conditional Distributions for Innovations

  • To model the excess kurtosis
  • Re-estimate the models with:
    • the Student Distribution
    • the Generalized Error Distribution (GED)

3. Check the Models

  • The Ljung-Box test on standardized residuals {\(\tilde{\epsilon}_t\)}
    • \(H_0\): adequate mean equation
  • The Ljung-Box test on standardized squared residuals {\(\tilde{\epsilon}_t^2\)}
    • \(H_0\): adequate volatility equation
  • If p-values > 0.05
    • Conclude that the mean equation and volatility equation are adequate

  • For example: GARCH(1,1)-Normal

Model Building: Stochastic Volatility

1. Prepare the Data

  • The data should be demeaned to avoid zero returns
    • To keep away from numerical errors

2. Specify Prior Distributions

  • For the level parameter \(\mu\):

    \[\mu\sim\mathcal{N}(-10,5)\]

  • For the persistence parameter \(\phi\):

    \[\frac{(\phi+1)}{2}\sim Beta(20,1.5)\]

    • The prior expectation is 0.8604651
    • The prior variance is 0.01153777

  • For the volatility of the log-volatility \(\sigma\):

    \[\sigma^2 \sim Gamma(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\]

3. Run the Sampler

  • Burn-in period: 1000 iterations
  • MCMC: 10000 draws

4. Assess the Output and Display the Results

  • The estimation of the stochastic volatility gives

\[ \begin{align*} y_t &= e^{h_t/2}\epsilon_t,\\ h_{t} &= -9.4697+0.9817(h_{t-1}+9.4697)+0.1746\eta_t\\ \end{align*} \]

  • The posterior mean of \(\phi\) is close to 1
    • Suggests a strong daily volatility persistence

5. Check the Model

  • Stochastic volatility:
    • Nonlinear
    • Involve latent variables
  • Infeasible to use standard diagnostic approaches to investigate the properties of residuals
    • Due to the latent dimension of stochastic volatility

  • Check the convergence of the MCMC by trace plots and density plots

  • Assess the model fit by Quantile-Quantile Plot

  • Concentrated around the means
    • Indicates good mixing of the Markov chain

  • Few outliers
    • The Q-Q plot suggests a good fit

Comparsion between GARCH & SV

Difference between GARCH & SV

GARCH model:

  • One white noise driving both conditional mean and conditional volatility
  • Estimation of parameters \((\mu,\omega,\alpha,\beta)\)
    • By maximizing the log likelihood

Stochastic volatility model:

  • Separate white noises driving the conditional mean and conditional volatility
    • Provides a more realistic and flexible model
    • A lot harder to estimate
  • Estimation of parameters \((\mu,\phi,\sigma)\)
    • By Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

Best Model for the S&P 500 Index

  • Measure the predictive accuracy by MAE and MSE
  • The true underlying volatility process is unobservable
    • \((y_t-\bar{y})^2\) is a proxy for the latent volatility

\[MAE=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}|(y_{t+n}-\bar{y})^2-\hat{h}_{t+n}|\]

\[MSE=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}[(y_{t+n}-\bar{y})^2-\hat{h}_{t+n}]^2\]

  • \(y_{t+n}\) is the asset return for \(n\)-steps ahead
  • \(\bar{y}\) is the mean of return
  • \(\hat{h}_{t+n}\) is the \(n\)-steps ahead forecasted volatility

  • \(39\)-days ahead volatility forecasts

Conclusion

Aim of the Project

  • Study volatility models:
    • GARCH, GARCH-M & stochastic volatility
  • Apply to real data
    • Procedure of model estimation
    • Application workflow

Results from the S&P 500 Analysis

  • GED is better than the normal distribution and the Student Distribution in GARCH models
  • GARCH-M models the data better than GARCH
    • The conditional variance can impact the mean of future returns
  • SV provides better out-of-sample forecast performance than all GARCH-type models

Problems

  • GARCH-type models are more frequently used than SV
    • SV: takes a long time to compute the MCMC algorithm (~3 min)
    • GARCH-type: computes the MLE quickly (~1-2 s)
  • The additional predictive accuracy of SV is not that attractive to people
    • Especially in the case of analyzing multiple large data sets
